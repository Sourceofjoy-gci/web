Primary Courts

Magistrate Courts of Eswatini

The cornerstone of justice delivery across the Kingdom

Overview

Magistrate Courts serve as the primary courts of first instance in Eswatini's judicial system. They handle the majority of civil and criminal cases, providing accessible justice to communities across the Kingdom. These courts play a vital role in maintaining law and order, resolving disputes, and ensuring that justice is delivered promptly and effectively at the local level.

Judicial Hierarchy

The Magistrate Courts are comprised of three levels of judicial officers, each with distinct roles and responsibilities:

Principal Magistrate

The highest-ranking magistrate who oversees the administration of all Magistrate Courts across Eswatini. The Principal Magistrate ensures consistent application of the law and effective court operations.

Senior Magistrate

Experienced judicial officers who handle more complex cases and provide guidance to magistrates. They often preside over matters in urban centers and assist the Principal Magistrate in administrative functions.

Magistrates

The backbone of the local court system, magistrates handle the day-to-day adjudication of cases. They are stationed throughout the country, ensuring that justice is accessible to all citizens.

Appointment Process

All magistrates are appointed by the Judicial Service Commission, ensuring the selection of qualified individuals with the necessary legal training and integrity to uphold justice.

Jurisdiction and Authority

Types of cases handled by Magistrate Courts

Criminal Jurisdiction

Magistrate Courts have jurisdiction over a wide range of criminal matters, focusing primarily on less serious offenses. This includes:

Minor Assaults

Common assault and assault with minor injuries

Theft

Petty theft and theft of property below certain value thresholds

Traffic Violations

Including dangerous driving, driving without a license, and related offenses

Public Disturbances

Including public disorder, noise violations, and public nuisance

Municipal Violations

Breaches of local bylaws and municipal regulations

Preliminary Hearings

Initial hearings for serious crimes before transfer to higher courts

Sentencing Powers

Magistrate Courts can impose various penalties including:

  • Fines up to specified amounts
  • Imprisonment for terms up to 5 years (depending on magistrate rank)
  • Community service orders
  • Probation and suspended sentences

Civil Jurisdiction

Magistrate Courts handle a variety of civil matters, providing accessible resolution for common disputes. Their civil jurisdiction includes:

Property Disputes

Small claims related to property boundaries, damages, and trespass

Contract Matters

Disputes arising from contracts below a certain monetary threshold

Debt Recovery

Claims for repayment of debts and enforcement of financial obligations

Family Matters

Including divorce, child custody, and maintenance

Small Claims

Minor civil claims for damages and compensation

Administrative Matters

Including name changes, identity document issues, and official certifications

Monetary Jurisdiction

Magistrate Courts can hear civil cases with claims up to specified monetary limits, depending on the rank of the presiding magistrate:

  • Principal Magistrate: Higher monetary threshold
  • Senior Magistrate: Intermediate monetary threshold
  • Magistrate: Lower monetary threshold

Jurisdictional Limitations

While Magistrate Courts handle a wide range of matters, certain cases fall outside their jurisdiction and are reserved for other courts:

Swazi National Courts

Matters involving Swazi customary law and traditional practices are handled by the Swazi National Courts, not Magistrate Courts.

Industrial Court

Employment and labor disputes fall under the exclusive jurisdiction of the Industrial Court and Industrial Court of Appeal.

High Court

Serious criminal cases (such as murder, treason), constitutional matters, and high-value civil claims are reserved for the High Court.

Monetary Ceiling

Civil claims exceeding the monetary jurisdiction of Magistrate Courts must be filed in the High Court. The specific monetary threshold is periodically revised by legal notice.

When a matter falls outside their jurisdiction, Magistrate Courts will advise the parties to approach the appropriate forum. In criminal matters that exceed their jurisdiction, Magistrates may conduct preliminary hearings before transferring the case to a higher court.

Understanding the Appeal Process

Either party may appeal to the High Court if they are dissatisfied with a Magistrate Court decision. The appeal process provides an important safeguard in the justice system, allowing for review of both the legal reasoning and procedural fairness of lower court decisions.

Time Limits

Appeals must typically be filed within 14 days of judgment in criminal cases and 30 days in civil cases. Extensions may be granted in exceptional circumstances.

Grounds for Appeal

Appeals may be based on errors of law, misinterpretation of evidence, procedural irregularities, or the severity/leniency of sentences in criminal matters.

Documentation Required

The appellant must file a notice of appeal clearly stating the grounds for appeal, accompanied by a copy of the lower court's judgment and relevant court records.

High Court Powers

The High Court may affirm the decision, reverse it, modify it, or remit the matter back to the Magistrate Court with directions for rehearing or further consideration.

In some instances, the execution of a Magistrate Court's judgment may be stayed (temporarily halted) pending the outcome of an appeal, particularly where immediate enforcement would cause irreparable harm to the appellant.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about Magistrate Courts

How do I file a case at the Magistrate Court?

To file a case, visit the registry of the Magistrate Court in your area during operating hours. The court clerk will guide you through the process and provide the necessary forms. For civil cases, you'll need to complete a summons form detailing your claim. For criminal complaints, you'll make a statement that will be referred to the police or prosecutor. While legal representation is not mandatory, it's advisable to consult with a lawyer for complex matters.

What are the costs involved in filing a civil case?

Filing fees vary depending on the nature and value of the claim. The court registry maintains a schedule of fees for different types of applications and processes. These may include filing fees, service fees, and hearing fees. In cases of financial hardship, you may apply for a waiver of court fees (in forma pauperis). Additionally, if you win your case, the court may order the other party to reimburse your court costs.

Do I need a lawyer to represent me in Magistrate Court?

Legal representation is not mandatory in Magistrate Courts, and many people represent themselves, especially in straightforward matters. The court procedures are designed to be accessible to non-lawyers. However, if your case involves complex legal issues, substantial sums of money, or serious criminal charges, professional legal representation is advisable. If you cannot afford a lawyer, you may qualify for legal aid services provided by non-governmental organizations or pro bono legal clinics.

How long does it take for a case to be resolved?

The duration varies significantly depending on the nature and complexity of the case, the court's caseload, and the cooperation of all parties involved. Simple matters might be resolved within a few weeks or months, while more complex cases could take longer. Contested divorces, for example, typically take several months. The court strives to handle matters efficiently, but thoroughness and fairness take precedence over speed. Case management procedures are in place to minimize unnecessary delays.

What should I bring with me when attending court?

When attending court, bring all documents relevant to your case, including the originals and copies for the court and other parties. This may include contracts, receipts, photographs, correspondence, medical reports, and any other evidence supporting your case. Also bring identification, court notices or summons you've received, and contact information for any witnesses. Arrive at least 30 minutes early to locate your courtroom and check in with court staff. Dress neatly and respectfully, and turn off or silence electronic devices before entering the courtroom.

Can I appeal if I disagree with the Magistrate's decision?

Yes, you can appeal to the High Court if you disagree with a Magistrate Court decision. Appeals must be filed within specific timeframes: typically 14 days for criminal cases and 30 days for civil matters. You'll need to complete a notice of appeal clearly stating the grounds for your appeal. The appeal process involves a review of the lower court's legal reasoning and procedures, not a complete rehearing of the case. The High Court may affirm, reverse, or modify the Magistrate Court's decision based on this review.
