About the High Court

The High Court of Judicature for Eswatini is a Superior Court that stands as a pillar of the Kingdom's judicial system. Established under Chapter VI of the Constitution, it serves as the court of first instance with original jurisdiction over significant civil and criminal matters throughout the nation.

Composition & Leadership

The High Court consists of the Chief Justice (ex officio), not less than four Justices of the High Court as prescribed by law, and other Justices of the Superior Court assigned by the Chief Justice. The most senior Justice is designated as the Principal Judge, who presides over the court and exercises specific functions delegated by the Chief Justice.

Specialized Divisions

Specialized Divisions of the High Court

The Chief Justice, in consultation with the Minister responsible for Justice and the President of the Swaziland Law Society, has established specialized divisions to ensure efficient and expert adjudication

Civil Division

The Civil Division handles all non-criminal disputes between private parties or entities, including contract disputes, property matters, personal injury claims, and family law issues.

Core Functions:

  • Adjudicating private disputes between individuals, businesses, or organizations
  • Hearing family law matters including divorce, custody, and maintenance
  • Resolving land and property disputes
  • Determining civil liability and awarding damages

Filing Information

Civil proceedings commence through the filing of a summons or application at the High Court Registry. The Registry is open weekdays from 8:30 AM to 4:00 PM.

Download Filing Guidelines

Criminal Division

The Criminal Division presides over serious criminal cases and has exclusive jurisdiction over capital offenses and other serious crimes such as treason, sedition, and murder.

Core Functions:

  • Conducting trials for serious criminal offenses
  • Hearing bail applications for serious offenses
  • Issuing search and arrest warrants in complex cases
  • Reviewing decisions of magistrates in criminal matters

Case Information

Criminal cases are typically brought to the High Court through indictment by the Director of Public Prosecutions after investigation by law enforcement agencies.

Criminal Procedure Guide

Constitutional Division

The Constitutional Division interprets and enforces the Constitution of Eswatini, determining the constitutionality of laws, regulations, and actions of public officials and institutions.

Core Functions:

  • Interpreting provisions of the Constitution
  • Determining constitutional validity of legislation
  • Protecting fundamental rights and freedoms
  • Resolving disputes between state organs

Application Process

Constitutional matters can be brought before the court through direct application or referral from other courts when constitutional questions arise in ongoing proceedings.

Constitutional Application Guide

Commercial Division

The Commercial Division specializes in business and commercial disputes, providing expertise in complex commercial matters to support economic development and business confidence.

Core Functions:

  • Resolving disputes between business entities
  • Handling corporate and securities law matters
  • Adjudicating intellectual property disputes
  • Supervising business rescue and liquidation proceedings

Case Management

The Commercial Division employs specialized case management techniques to ensure efficient resolution of business disputes, often with expedited timelines.

Commercial Court Guidelines

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about the High Court

What types of cases does the High Court handle?

The High Court has original jurisdiction over all civil and criminal matters. It handles serious criminal cases like murder and treason, complex civil matters, constitutional questions, and appeals from lower courts. The court also supervises all subordinate courts and reviews administrative decisions.

How do I file a case at the High Court?

To file a case, you need to prepare the appropriate documents (such as a summons or application) according to the High Court Rules, pay the required filing fee at the High Court Registry, and ensure all documents are properly served on the other parties. It's advisable to seek legal assistance, as procedures can be complex and technical.

What are the costs involved in High Court proceedings?

Costs include filing fees (which vary depending on the type and value of the case), service fees, attorney fees if represented, and potentially additional costs for expert witnesses or special applications. The court may order the losing party to pay the winner's costs, though this is at the court's discretion.

How long do High Court cases typically take?

Case duration varies significantly depending on complexity, court schedules, and the actions of parties. Simple matters might be resolved in a few months, while complex litigation can take a year or longer. The court has implemented case management procedures to improve efficiency and reduce delays.

Do I need a lawyer to represent me in the High Court?

While it's not technically required, legal representation is strongly recommended due to the complexity of High Court procedures and legal principles. Self-represented litigants may face significant challenges navigating the strict procedural requirements. The court does provide some guidance for self-represented litigants but cannot provide legal advice.

How can I appeal a High Court decision?

Appeals from the High Court are made to the Supreme Court. You must file a notice of appeal within 30 days of the judgment and follow the specific requirements in the Supreme Court Rules. Appeals are generally limited to questions of law rather than re-examining factual findings.
