The High Court of Eswatini

The High Court of Eswatini is a superior court established by the Constitution with unlimited original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters, as well as appellate and review jurisdiction over subordinate courts and other adjudicating bodies.

Located in Mbabane, the High Court serves as a critical component in the Kingdom's judicial structure, ensuring the rule of law and upholding constitutional rights.

Jurisdiction & Powers

Original Jurisdiction

The High Court has unlimited original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters throughout Eswatini. This includes power to hear and determine any constitutional, civil, or criminal proceedings.

Appellate Jurisdiction

The Court hears appeals from decisions of subordinate courts, including the Magistrate's Courts, Small Claims Courts, and specialized tribunals operating within the Kingdom.

Review Jurisdiction

The High Court has the power to review decisions, judgments, and proceedings of lower courts to ensure procedural fairness and proper application of the law.

Constitutional Matters

The Court has jurisdiction to hear and determine matters relating to the interpretation and enforcement of provisions of the Constitution of Eswatini.

Starting the court process

Case Filing

A matter is initiated through filing of appropriate documents with the High Court Registry. This includes:

  • Filing the main application or summons
  • Paying required filing fees
  • Providing necessary supporting documents
Timeframe

Typically takes 1-2 days for processing

2

Case Assessment

Case review and allocation

Case Assessment

Case is assessed, assigned a number, and allocated to the appropriate division of the court. This includes:

  • Initial review by the Registrar
  • Assignment of a case number
  • Routing to the appropriate court division
Timeframe

1-3 weeks depending on case complexity

3

Pleadings

Exchange of legal documents

Pleadings

Exchange of formal written statements setting out claims and defenses between parties, including:

  • Statement of claim/defense
  • Counter-claims if applicable
  • Reply to defense when necessary
Timeframe

14-30 days for each response

4

Pre-Trial

Preparation and settlement attempts

Pre-Trial

Pre-trial conference to clarify issues, explore settlement options, and prepare for trial, including:

  • Discovery of documents
  • Settlement negotiations
  • Witness arrangements
Timeframe

1-3 months depending on complexity

5

Trial

Hearing of the case

Trial

Formal hearing where evidence is presented, witnesses testify, and arguments are made, including:

  • Opening statements
  • Presentation of evidence
  • Cross-examination
  • Closing arguments
Timeframe

1 day to several weeks

6

Judgment

Court decision

Judgment

Court delivers judgment based on evidence and legal arguments presented, including:

  • Finding of facts
  • Application of relevant law
  • Court orders and remedies
Timeframe

2 weeks to 3 months after trial concludes

7

Final phase

Appeal/Execution

Judgment enforced or appealed to Supreme Court within prescribed time limits, including:

  • Appeal filing (within 6 weeks)
  • Execution of judgment
  • Collection of damages/enforcement of orders

Appeal process: 6-18 months
Execution: Varies by case

Court Divisions

Specialized divisions to efficiently handle different types of cases

Civil Division

The Civil Division handles a wide range of non-criminal matters including:

  • Contract and commercial disputes
  • Property and land matters
  • Debt recovery cases
  • Personal injury claims
  • Administrative law and judicial reviews
  • Constitutional matters

Civil cases are typically initiated through summons or applications and follow formal procedures with opportunities for alternative dispute resolution where appropriate.

Criminal Division

The Criminal Division adjudicates serious criminal offenses including:

  • Murder and homicide cases
  • Serious assault and violent crimes
  • Sexual offenses
  • Major fraud and financial crimes
  • High-value theft and robbery
  • Treason and offenses against the state

Criminal proceedings in the High Court follow established rules of criminal procedure with strict protections for the rights of the accused while ensuring justice for victims.

Commercial Division

The Commercial Division specializes in business-related matters including:

  • Complex business and commercial disputes
  • Corporate and shareholder litigation
  • Banking and financial services disputes
  • Intellectual property matters
  • Insurance claims
  • Bankruptcy and insolvency proceedings

This specialized division provides efficient and expert handling of complex business disputes with an appreciation for commercial realities and implications.

Family Division

The Family Division handles domestic and family-related matters including:

  • Divorce and matrimonial proceedings
  • Child custody and guardianship
  • Maintenance and alimony
  • Adoption proceedings
  • Domestic violence protection orders
  • Family property disputes

The Family Division approaches cases with sensitivity to family dynamics while prioritizing the best interests of children and vulnerable family members.

Filing Requirements

Essential requirements for filing cases in the High Court

Documents Required

  • Original application or summons
  • Supporting affidavits (where applicable)
  • Annexures and evidence documents
  • Notice of motion (for applications)
  • Certificate of urgency (for urgent matters)

Filing Fees

  • Civil matters: E300 - E1,500 (depending on claim value)
  • Criminal matters: No filing fees for accused
  • Application fees: E250 - E500
  • Urgent application surcharge: E200
  • Appeal filing fees: E500

* Fee waiver available for indigent litigants

Time Limitations

  • Civil claims: Within 3 years of cause of action
  • Replies to pleadings: 14 days from service
  • Appeals from Magistrate Court: 6 weeks from judgment
  • Review applications: 6 months from decision
  • Constitutional matters: No specific time limit

Procedural Requirements

  • Proper service of documents on all parties
  • Correct citation of parties
  • Compliance with High Court Rules
  • Proper form and format of pleadings
  • Sufficient copies for court and all parties

For detailed information on filing requirements and procedures, please consult with the High Court Registry or seek legal advice from a qualified legal practitioner.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I file a case in the High Court?

To file a case in the High Court, you need to prepare the appropriate documents (summons, application, or notice of motion), pay the required filing fees at the Registry, and submit the correct number of copies. It's advisable to seek legal assistance from an attorney who can guide you through the specific requirements for your type of case.

What is the difference between the High Court and Magistrate Courts?

The High Court has unlimited original jurisdiction in both civil and criminal matters, while Magistrate Courts have limited jurisdiction based on the value of claims or severity of offenses. The High Court also serves as an appellate court for cases from Magistrate Courts. Certain serious cases can only be heard by the High Court.

How long does it take for a case to be concluded?

The duration of cases varies widely depending on complexity, nature of the matter, court schedules, and procedural developments. Simple matters might be resolved within months, while complex litigation can take years. The court is working to improve efficiency and reduce delays, but parties should be prepared for the process to take time.

Can I represent myself in the High Court?

Yes, self-representation (appearing in person) is permitted in the High Court. However, due to the complex procedures and legal principles involved, it's generally advisable to seek legal representation. If you cannot afford an attorney, you may qualify for legal aid through the Legal Aid Clinic or pro bono services from the Law Society.

How can I appeal a High Court decision?

To appeal a High Court decision, you must file a Notice of Appeal with the High Court Registry within 6 weeks of the judgment or order being appealed. The appeal will be heard by the Supreme Court of Eswatini. The notice must specify the grounds of appeal and be accompanied by the prescribed fee. It's highly recommended to seek legal advice for appeals.
