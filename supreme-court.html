Apex Court

The Supreme Court of Eswatini

The final arbiter of justice and guardian of the Constitution

EST. 1954

Historical Foundation

The Supreme Court of Eswatini traces its origins to the Court of Appeal Act of 1954, with its modern form and authority firmly established in Sections 145 and 146 of the Constitution. As the nation's highest judicial authority, it serves as the final interpreter of law and ultimate guardian of constitutional rights.

The Supreme Court stands as the final bastion of justice, where the law finds its definitive interpretation and constitutional principles are upheld with unwavering resolve.

The Role of the Supreme Court

As the highest court in the Kingdom, the Supreme Court serves several critical functions

Final Appellate Authority

The Supreme Court is the court of last instance in Eswatini, hearing appeals from the High Court in both its original and appellate jurisdictions.

Final word on civil appeals Ultimate arbiter in criminal appeals No further appeals possible

Constitutional Guardian

The Court has special jurisdiction to interpret and protect the Constitution, ensuring all laws and government actions conform to constitutional principles.

Final interpreter of the Constitution Protector of constitutional rights Authority on constitutional disputes

Supervisory Powers

The Supreme Court exercises oversight over all lower courts in Eswatini, ensuring proper application of the law and adherence to judicial standards.

Oversight of all courts Review authority Sets legal precedents

Special Jurisdiction

Beyond appellate cases, the Supreme Court has additional jurisdiction conferred by the Constitution and other laws for matters of exceptional significance.

Special constitutional matters Issues of national importance Matters affecting government powers

The Supreme Court of Judicature for Eswatini consists of:

  • The Chief Justice
  • Not less than four other Justices of the Supreme Court

Full Bench

For matters of exceptional importance, a full bench consists of:

5

Five Justices of the Supreme Court

Presiding Justice

When the Chief Justice is present, he or she presides over sittings of the Supreme Court.

In the absence of the Chief Justice, the most senior Justice among those constituting the court presides.

Understanding the Appeal Process

How cases reach the Supreme Court and what happens when they do

01

Decision in Lower Court

The process begins with a judgment or order from the High Court (either in its original jurisdiction or from an appeal from a magistrate's court).

02

Filing Notice of Appeal

A party dissatisfied with the High Court decision must file a Notice of Appeal with the Registrar of the Supreme Court within strict time limits, typically 30 days from the judgment date.

03

Record Preparation

The Registrar prepares the appeal record, which includes all relevant documents from the lower court proceedings, transcripts, and exhibits.

04

Filing Written Submissions

Both appellant and respondent file written arguments (called "heads of argument") outlining their legal positions, relevant precedents, and desired outcomes.

05

Hearing

The appeal is heard by a panel of at least three Justices (or five for a full bench). Legal representatives present oral arguments and answer questions from the bench.

06

Deliberation

The Justices deliberate on the case in private, considering the written and oral arguments, lower court record, and relevant law.

07

Judgment

The Supreme Court issues its final decision, which may affirm, reverse, or modify the lower court's judgment. This decision is final and binding on all courts and persons in Eswatini.

1 / 7

The Supreme Court typically sits in sessions during certain periods of the year. These sessions are announced in advance to allow parties and their representatives to prepare. Emergency appeals may be heard outside the regular sessions at the discretion of the Chief Justice.

Legal Significance of Supreme Court Decisions

Understanding the impact and importance of judgments from the highest court

Binding Precedent

Supreme Court decisions establish binding precedents that must be followed by all lower courts in Eswatini. This ensures consistency and predictability in the legal system.

Constitutional Clarity

As the final interpreter of the Constitution, the Supreme Court provides definitive guidance on constitutional questions, shaping the fundamental rights and governance structures of the nation.

Legal Development

Through its judgments, the Supreme Court develops and refines the law, adapting legal principles to contemporary social, economic, and technological changes while maintaining core values.

Finality

Supreme Court decisions provide closure and certainty to legal disputes. The finality of these judgments is essential for the rule of law and the effective functioning of the justice system.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the Supreme Court of Eswatini

How often does the Supreme Court sit?

The Supreme Court typically sits in sessions or "terms" throughout the year, usually quarterly. The exact dates are announced by the Chief Justice. These sessions may last for several weeks, during which multiple appeals are heard. In cases of urgency, special sittings may be arranged outside the regular sessions.

Can anyone appeal to the Supreme Court?

Not all cases can be appealed to the Supreme Court. Generally, a person must first have exhausted appeals at the High Court level. In some instances, leave (permission) to appeal may be required. Appeals are typically limited to questions of law rather than reassessment of factual findings. Additionally, there are strict time limits for filing appeals.

How long does it take for the Supreme Court to decide a case?

The time frame varies depending on the complexity of the case, the court's schedule, and other factors. Some judgments may be delivered immediately after the hearing, while others, especially in complex matters, might be "reserved," with the written judgment issued weeks or months later. The Court strives to deliver judgments within a reasonable time to ensure justice is not delayed.

Are Supreme Court hearings open to the public?

Yes, Supreme Court hearings are generally open to the public as part of the principle of open justice. However, in exceptional circumstances, the Court may order closed proceedings if necessary for justice, national security, public morality, or the protection of vulnerable persons. Advance notice of upcoming cases is typically published, and interested members of the public can attend hearings as observers.

What happens if the Supreme Court Justices disagree?

When Justices disagree, decisions are made by majority vote. Each Justice may write their own judgment, resulting in a majority opinion and one or more dissenting opinions. While the majority opinion becomes the binding decision, dissenting opinions are also published and form part of the legal record. These dissents can be influential in the development of the law over time and may inform future cases.

Where can I find Supreme Court judgments?

Supreme Court judgments are public documents and are officially published in the Eswatini Law Reports. They may also be available through the Judiciary website, legal databases, the High Court Registry, and law libraries. Recent and significant judgments are sometimes summarized in press releases issued by the Judiciary. Legal practitioners can access the full text of judgments through their professional networks and resources.
