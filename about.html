Judicial Power

Judicial power of Eswatini vests in the Judiciary. Accordingly, an organ or agency of the Crown shall not have or be conferred with final judicial power. In the exercise of the judicial power under the Constitution or any other law, the superior courts may, in relation to any matter within their jurisdiction, issue such orders or directions as may be necessary to ensure the enforcement of any judgement, decree or order of those courts.

The Judiciary is independent and subject only to this Constitution, and shall not be subject to the control or direction of any person or authority. Neither the Crown nor Parliament shall have the power to control the exercise of judicial power.

A member of the Judiciary shall be independent, impartial and subject only to this Constitution and the law. The Judiciary shall not accept or be directed to accept any authority, direction or control from any person or body except as provided in this Constitution or as prescribed by law not inconsistent with this Constitution.

Judiciary Structure

The Judiciary is responsible for the administration of Justice, the upholding of the rule of law and the administration of estates. The Judiciary carries out its constitutional responsibilities through the following departments:

Apex Court

Supreme Court

Final appellate court in the land and hears appeals from the High Court

Superior Courts

High Court

Has original, review and appellate jurisdiction; tries and reviews cases from lower courts

Industrial Court of Appeal

Hears appeals from the Industrial Court

Specialized & First Instance

Magistrate Courts

Courts of first instance in all matters, both civil and criminal, except those reserved for other courts

Industrial Court

Responsible for the hearing of Labour disputes

Small Claims Court

Handles cases under E10,000 without requiring legal representation

Swazi National Courts

Traditional courts administered by the Office of the Judicial Commissioner

Administrative Offices

Master of the High Court

Administers deceased estates, liquidation of companies and insolvent estates

Judicial Commissioner

Responsible for oversight of the Swazi National Courts

Court Jurisdiction

Each court in Eswatini's judiciary has specific jurisdiction defined by law:

  • Supreme Court: Final appellate jurisdiction over all matters from the High Court
  • High Court: Unlimited original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters; appellate jurisdiction from subordinate courts
  • Magistrate Courts: Limited civil jurisdiction based on claim value; criminal jurisdiction except for capital offenses
  • Industrial Court: Exclusive jurisdiction over labor disputes and employment matters
  • Small Claims Court: Civil claims not exceeding E10,000
  • Swazi National Courts: Matters governed by Swazi Law and Custom

Appeals Process

Eswatini's judiciary has a clearly defined appeals hierarchy:

First Instance Courts
High Court / Industrial Court of Appeal
Supreme Court

Appeals must generally be based on errors of law or procedure rather than simple disagreement with the outcome.

Specialized Courts

Our judiciary includes specialized courts to handle specific types of cases:

Industrial Court

Small Claims Court

Swazi National Courts

Chief Justice Administrative Functions

The Chief Justice is the head of the Judiciary and is responsible for the administration and supervision of the courts. The administrative functions of the Chief Justice include:

Performance Monitoring

Monitoring the overall performance of the courts and establishing performance standards

Administrative Procedures

Setting up administrative procedures to expedite the disposal of matters in the courts

Annual Reporting

Preparing annual reports on the operations of the Judiciary

Recommendations

Making recommendations for the improvement of the administration of justice

Supervision

Supervising judicial officers and staff of the Judiciary

JSC Leadership

Acting as chairperson of the Judicial Service Commission

Independence of the Judiciary

In the Kingdom of Eswatini, the Judiciary is vested with judicial power and shall be independent and subject only to the Constitution. Neither the Crown nor Parliament shall have the power to control the exercise of judicial power.

The essence of judicial independence lies in the freedom of a judicial officer to hear and decide cases without external pressure or influence and with only the law and conscience as guides. This principle is foundational to the rule of law and the protection of the rights of all persons within the jurisdiction.

Judicial Independence

Key aspects of judicial independence in Eswatini include:

Institutional independence

The Judiciary as an institution is separate from the executive and legislative branches of government.

Individual independence

Individual judges and judicial officers are protected from improper influences in their decision-making.

Financial autonomy

The Judiciary has a separate budget allocation to ensure operational independence.

Security of tenure

Judicial officers have security of tenure, protecting them from arbitrary removal.

Administrative independence

The Judiciary has control over administrative decisions that directly affect the exercise of its judicial function.

History of the Judiciary in Eswatini

The judicial system in Eswatini reflects the country's dual legal heritage, combining elements of Roman-Dutch common law with traditional Swazi customary law. The evolution of the judiciary in Eswatini can be traced through several distinct periods:

Pre-Colonial Era

Prior to colonial influence, disputes in Swazi society were resolved through a hierarchical system of traditional courts, with the King serving as the final arbiter of justice. This system emphasized restorative justice, community harmony, and respect for traditional authority.

Colonial Period (1903-1968)

During the British colonial administration, a formal Western-style court system was introduced, operating alongside the traditional courts. This dual system recognized the importance of preserving indigenous legal customs while incorporating elements of British justice.

Post-Independence Development

After independence in 1968, Eswatini continued to develop its judicial framework, establishing the High Court and Court of Appeal (later the Supreme Court) while maintaining the role of traditional courts in appropriate matters.

Constitutional Era

The adoption of the 2005 Constitution marked a significant milestone in the development of the judiciary, formalizing the principle of judicial independence and establishing a clear hierarchical structure of courts.

Modern Judiciary

Today's judiciary continues to evolve, embracing modern technologies and practices while honoring the country's legal traditions and cultural heritage.

Throughout its history, the Eswatini judiciary has continually evolved to meet the changing needs of society while preserving the valuable aspects of traditional justice that reflect the country's unique cultural heritage.
